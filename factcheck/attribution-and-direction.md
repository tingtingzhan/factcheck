# Attribution & direction — who did what, to whom

Guards for assigning acts to parties correctly: cause on a shared ledger, the
direction of every transfer/request, per-item roles that don't carry across
siblings, multi-actor tallies that don't collapse onto one party, and verbs that
assert a broken commitment.


>[!caution] Cause
>
>When assigning cause, enter the focal party's own acts on the same ledger as counterparties' acts.
>
>Recoding a party's acts as purely "reactive/defensive" is permitted only with a cited antecedent hostile act that precedes each; absent that, count the act as a contributing cause, not an excused response.


>[!caution] Agency & Direction
>
>Before asserting who does/provides/refuses what, verify the **direction** against the source record: which party is the source and which is the recipient.
>
>Do not infer direction from plausibility or from a party's role label.
>
>Claims about who supplied, transferred, requested, or delivered what must name the source and recipient party and cite the passage in the record establishing it.


>[!caution] Per-Item Attribution (no carryover across siblings)
>
>When a role, credit, status, or attribute is assigned item-by-item — per manuscript, per grant, per contract, per party — verify it against the **specific item** before asserting it.
>
>A role held on item A is not evidence of the same role on item B, even when they share a context or one party appears in both. Do not let a shared attribute complete the pattern onto its sibling.
>
>Cite the passage that fixes the attribute *for the item at hand*, not for a neighbor.


>[!caution] Aggregate Pattern → Single Party (don't collapse a multi-actor tally onto one)
>
>When the record states a *pattern* spanning several instances, verify **which party** owns **each** instance before attributing the count to anyone.
>
>A tally of $N$ events across multiple actors is not evidence that any single actor did $N$. Attribute only the instance(s) whose actor the record names for that instance; cite the passage fixing the actor *per instance*, not the summary sentence that reports the pattern.


>[!important] Broken Commitment Verb
>
>Any verb implying a broken commitment ("renege", "back out", "abandon", "reverse", "foreclose", "held-until", "triggered-by", etc.) must specify
>- _what_ was reversed
>- owed _to whom_
>- known _to whom_.
>
>Any claim that *a disposition "held until" an act "foreclosed" it* must verify that the foreclosing act _precedes_ the conduct it allegedly foreclosed.


>[!caution] Interval & Temporal Anchor (name both endpoints; don't invent one)
>
>Before asserting that one event happened *"$N$ units before / after"* another, or that a span *"$T$ elapsed between"* two events, name **both** anchor events and verify each is fixed on the record — its occurrence *and*, where the claim depends on it, its timing.
>
>- **Do not invent an anchor.** An event the record only *proposes, requests, or leaves open-ended* ("as soon as possible", "to be scheduled") is not a fixed point in time. Do not manufacture a scheduled or dated event to serve as the endpoint an interval is measured to.
>- **Read direction off the record, not off plausibility.** Which event is earlier and which later comes from the record's own timestamps, never from the more dramatic or expected ordering. "X shortly before Y" and "Y shortly after X" are different claims; assert only the one the record fixes.
>- **Attach the interval to the pair the record actually dates.** When several events cluster, verify *which two* the elapsed time spans before reporting it; an interval between A and B is not evidence of the same interval between A and C.
>
>This is the ordering-analogue of *Default-State Smuggling*: there, don't assume an artifact is *still there*; here, don't assume an event is *fixed in time* — or that it exists at all — merely because it was mooted.


>[!caution] Distinguish Repeated Same-Type Events Before Attaching a Cause
>
>When a record contains **several events of the same kind** (multiple releases, filings, emails, payments, meetings, versions), do not merge them into one "the $X$." Each instance carries **distinguishing keys** — date, channel/venue, actor, target, version. Fix the keys of the *specific* instance before asserting anything about it; a property of one instance (its motive, effect, trigger, audience) is not a property of its siblings.
>
>**Then gate the cause to that instance's own timeline.** A motive or trigger may be attached to an instance only if its antecedent is on record **as of that instance's date** — not merely somewhere in the story. Explaining an *earlier* instance with facts that became true only *later* (a demand, an escalation, a status change dated after it) is backdating a cause: the same error as the Anachronism Guard, applied to *why an act happened* rather than *what a party could do*. If the antecedent post-dates the instance, it cannot be its cause — find the instance the antecedent actually precedes, or retract the causal claim.
>
>Corollary — **channel/venue is load-bearing, not decoration.** The same act through a different channel can have a different reach or meaning (a private draft vs. a public authoritative registry; an internal note vs. a filed complaint). When two instances differ only by channel, that difference is often the analytic point — do not let it wash out in the merge.
