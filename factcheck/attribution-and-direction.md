# Attribution & direction — who did what, to whom

Guards for assigning acts to the right party, in the right direction, at the right time.


>[!caution] Cause
>
>When assigning cause, enter the focal party's own acts on the same ledger as counterparties' acts. Recoding a party's acts as purely "reactive/defensive" is permitted only with a cited antecedent hostile act preceding each; absent that, count the act as a contributing cause, not an excused response.


>[!caution] Agency & Direction
>
>Before asserting who does/provides/refuses what, verify the **direction** against the record: which party is source, which is recipient. Do not infer direction from plausibility or a party's role label. Claims about who supplied, transferred, requested, or delivered what must name source and recipient and cite the passage establishing it.


>[!caution] Supply-Status Is the Provider's Act, Not the Recipient's Need (dependence ≠ still-flowing)
>
>Before asserting that a provided or relational good — support, access, cooperation, funding, protection, sustaining labor — is **still flowing** or **already cut**, fix its status from the **provider's latest act on record**, never from the **recipient's** continued need, dependence, presence, or reduced need. Provision and its withdrawal are the **provider's** act; need is the **recipient's** state — different facts, different owners, and the record can fix them in opposite directions at once.
>
>- **Dependence does not establish availability.** "Still in the relationship / still needs it / still relies on it / hasn't left" does not show the good is still supplied — a party can still need what has already been cut off. A still-flowing claim rests on the provider's most recent act (continued, paused, refused, terminated), not on the recipient's stake.
>- **Reduced need does not establish withdrawal.** Symmetrically, "no longer needs it" does not show it was withdrawn; the good may remain on offer while unused.
>- **Who holds the on/off switch is a direction question.** Identify the provider (source) before ranging over status (→ *Agency & Direction*), then read *that* party's latest act at the point of use (→ *Default-State Smuggling* / *Lapse Smuggling*: status is last-mention-wins, retrieved not recalled). Inferring supply-status from the recipient's situation skips both steps.



>[!caution] Per-Item Attribution (no carryover across siblings)
>
>When a role, credit, status, or attribute is assigned item-by-item — per manuscript, grant, contract, party — verify it against the **specific item** before asserting.  A role held on item $A$ is not evidence of the same role on item $B$, even when they share a context or one party appears in both.   Don't let a shared attribute complete the pattern onto its sibling. Cite the passage that fixes the attribute *for the item at hand*, not a neighbor.
>
>**A relational attribute belongs to the pair, not to the shared endpoint.** A relationship — warm, hostile, trusting, technical — is a property of the *two parties together*. That one party holds tie $W$ with $A$ is not evidence the same party holds $W$ with $B$; the common party makes the carryover feel automatic, which is the trap. Read the tie from the record of *that* pair.


>[!caution] Aggregate Pattern → Single Party (don't collapse a multi-actor tally onto one)
>
>When the record states a *pattern* spanning several instances, verify **which party** owns **each** instance before attributing the count. A tally of $N$ events across multiple actors is not evidence any single actor did $N$. Attribute only the instance(s) whose actor the record names for that instance; cite the passage fixing the actor *per instance*, not the summary sentence reporting the pattern.


>[!important] Broken Commitment Verb
>
>Any verb implying a broken commitment ("renege", "back out", "abandon", "reverse", "foreclose", "held-until", "triggered-by", etc.) must specify _what_ was reversed, owed _to whom_, and known _to whom_. Any claim that a disposition "held until" an act "foreclosed" it must verify the foreclosing act _precedes_ the conduct it allegedly foreclosed.


>[!caution] Interval & Temporal Anchor (name both endpoints; don't invent one)
>
>Before asserting one event happened *"$N$ units before/after"* another, or that *"$T$ elapsed between"* two events, name **both** anchors and verify each is fixed on the record — its occurrence *and*, where the claim depends on it, its timing.
>
>- **Don't invent an anchor.** An event the record only *proposes, requests, or leaves open-ended* ("as soon as possible", "to be scheduled") is not a fixed point. Don't manufacture a scheduled/dated event to serve as an endpoint.
>- **Read direction off the record, not plausibility.** Which event is earlier comes from the record's timestamps, never the more dramatic ordering. "$X$ shortly before $Y$" and "$Y$ shortly after $X$" are different claims; assert only the one the record fixes.
>- **Attach the interval to the pair the record actually dates.** When events cluster, verify *which two* the span covers; an interval between $A$ and $B$ is not evidence of the same between $A$ and $C$.
>
>This is the ordering-analogue of *Default-State Smuggling*: there, don't assume an artifact is *still there*; here, don't assume an event is *fixed in time* — or exists at all — merely because it was mooted.



>[!caution] Read the Full Span — don't shrink an open-ended reach to the nearest endpoint
>
>Before treating a demand, obligation, dependence, or commitment as **one-off or bounded**, retrieve the reach the record fixes and carry it whole. When the wording extends it **forward or open-endedly** ("and the next", "in future", "ongoing", "indefinitely", "thereafter"), a nearby deadline is one point inside its span, not its end.
>
>- **The salient near-term event is not the scope.** An imminent endpoint pulls the eye; check whether the item's own wording reaches past it before scoping the item to it.
>- **Reach flips the downstream verdict.** A bounded item may be dischargeable, one-time, or self-curable; the same item at its true open-ended reach is none of these. Price it at the span on record.
>- **A backward-looking cure can't close a forward-reaching span.** A remedy settling the past portion doesn't satisfy a reach extending into the future; match the cure's direction to the span's (→ *Lapse Smuggling*).
>
>(Companion to *Interval & Temporal Anchor*: there, don't invent or mis-attach an endpoint; here, don't amputate the far end of a span the record fixes.)



>[!caution] Continuity ≠ Transition (don't invent the event between two states)
>
>When a passage reports one thing **ending, lapsing, or becoming unavailable** alongside another thing **remaining, persisting, or still in place**, that juxtaposition fixes **two states**. It does not fix a **transition** between them. Before asserting a party switched, migrated, replaced, or moved from A to B, cite the passage that records the **act** — not two facts sitting next to each other.
>
>- **Read the record's own aspect marker.** Verbs like *remains, still, continues, unchanged, has always* mark **continuity** and positively exclude a change; *becomes, switched to, replaced, moved* mark a transition. Don't overwrite the first with the second.
>- **A manufactured transition imports an actor and a motive.** A state that never changed has no agent and no reason; a switch has both. Inventing the event silently hands some party an action to explain — the error is not neutral, it manufactures agency (→ *Agency & Direction*).
>- **It also inverts the fact's significance.** "Unaffected by the change in X" and "adjusted in response to X" are opposite claims about the same pair of states, and they license opposite downstream readings. If the record says the second thing was never coupled to the first, a transition claim asserts exactly the coupling the record denies.
>
>(Companion to *Interval & Temporal Anchor*: there, don't invent an endpoint's **timing**; here, don't invent the **event** itself out of two adjacent states.)




>[!caution] Distinguish Repeated Same-Type Events Before Attaching a Cause
>
>When a record contains **several events of the same kind** (releases, filings, emails, payments, meetings, versions), don't merge them into one "the $X$." Each carries **distinguishing keys** — date, channel/venue, actor, target, version. Fix the keys of the *specific* instance before asserting anything; a property of one instance (motive, effect, trigger, audience) is not a property of its siblings.
>
>**Then gate the cause to that instance's own timeline.** A motive or trigger attaches to an instance only if its antecedent is on record **as of that instance's date** — not merely somewhere in the story. Explaining an *earlier* instance with facts that became true only *later* (a demand, escalation, status change dated after it) is backdating a cause: the Anachronism Guard error applied to *why an act happened*. If the antecedent post-dates the instance, it cannot be its cause — find the instance it actually precedes, or retract the causal claim.
>
>Corollary — **channel/venue is load-bearing, not decoration.** The same act through a different channel can have different reach or meaning (private draft vs. public registry; internal note vs. filed complaint). When two instances differ only by channel, that difference is often the analytic point — don't let it wash out in the merge.
